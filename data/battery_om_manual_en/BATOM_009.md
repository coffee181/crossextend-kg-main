| T1 | During a heater-loop complaint on Kestrel PackHeat-506, capture the command state of the isolation valve, the inlet and outlet temperatures, and whether the observed issue is no warm-up, no change in flow path, or a valve stuck between positions. Record the operating mode that produced the fault before disconnecting the actuator. |
| T2 | Expose the isolation valve body, actuator connector, bracket ear, inlet hose, outlet hose, bypass branch, and any nearby NTC lead so the electrical and thermal boundaries can be read together. Keep the hose routing unchanged until the as-found valve angle and bracket preload are noted. |
| T3 | Backprobe the actuator connector during a commanded valve event and record supply, ground, and control transition, then compare those values with the audible or tactile response at the valve housing. A commanded but silent valve belongs to a different boundary than a valve that clicks yet leaves the hose temperatures unchanged. |
| T4 | Compare inlet-to-outlet temperature change and bypass-branch behavior during command transitions while watching whether the valve housing shifts in the bracket or the hoses load the body sideways. This thermal comparison proves whether the spool is actually moving coolant or only reporting a command change. |
| T5 | If electrical behavior is correct but flow does not redirect, inspect the bracket ear, hose twist, contamination at the valve ports, and the actuator-to-body joint before condemning the valve assembly. A distorted bracket can make the actuator look weak while the real problem is mechanical bind at the valve body. |
| T6 | Correct only the failed actuator, spool assembly, connector feed, bracket preload, or hose blockage, then rerun the same heating command and compare both electrical evidence and temperature response with the baseline. The repeated test must be performed in the same mode that originally showed the missing warm-up support. |
| T7 | Recheck the valve body, actuator shaft, hose clamps, and bracket ear after the repeated command cycle because a restored flow path is not acceptable if the housing starts to sweat coolant or the connector backs out once the branch warms. This post-test inspection anchors the repair to the final mounted state. |
| T8 | Finish the valve diagnosis only when the report states the exact fault boundary, the measured command evidence, the observed hose-temperature change, and the final physical condition from actuator connector to outlet hose. The accepted state is a valve branch that redirects flow on command, stays dry, and remains mechanically neutral in its bracket through the repeated heating cycle. |
