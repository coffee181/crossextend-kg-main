| T1 | On Trion PackVolt-418 LR, document whether corrosion was found during routine inspection, after an insulation warning, or after water ingress near the output studs, and photograph both positive and negative studs before removing boots or nuts. Note the pack orientation and any evidence of moisture path above the stud plane so the corrosion map can be traced back to a source. |
| T2 | Safe the pack and expose the HV output studs, copper ring lugs, Belleville washers, insulating plates, boot seals, and cover drain path while preserving the original hardware order. Keep the positive and negative stacks visible together because asymmetry between them often reveals the real corrosion driver. |
| T3 | Map corrosion color, spread, and depth on the exposed threads, nut faces, lug clamp faces, and nearby aluminum or plated steel surfaces, distinguishing surface oxidation from contamination trapped inside the current-carrying joint. Deposits only on exposed threads do not carry the same risk as oxide between lug and stud shoulder. |
| T4 | Measure joint voltage drop or low-resistance value across each stud stack if the procedure allows it, then compare those readings with the visible corrosion map and the clamp witness under each washer. A stud that looks dirty but measures normally belongs to a different risk class than one with only slight staining and elevated drop. |
| T5 | Inspect the boot sealing lip, cover drain path, washer imprint, and lug flatness to determine whether the corrosion entered from above, from poor boot sealing, or from clamp-load loss that allowed fretting at the interface. The location of clean witness marks is often more informative than the size of the stain itself. |
| T6 | Compare the positive stack and the negative stack for hardware order, washer spring height, boot compression, and local water path so the root cause lands on one boot, one drain edge, or one clamp stack rather than on the pack generally. Record any meaningful difference between the two sides explicitly. |
| T7 | Restore only the affected stud hardware, sealing boot, drain path, or lug face condition, then reassemble the stack in the verified order and confirm that the cable leaves the stud without twisting the boot or side-loading the insulator plate. The rebuilt stack must sit flat before any closeout claim is made. |
| T8 | Recheck clamp witness, boot seating, and electrical condition after final loading, because a stack that looks correct before compression can still shift once the nut reaches its final torque. This last check anchors the repair to the true assembled state of the stud joint. |
| T9 | Finish the corrosion review only when the report names the exact affected item, such as positive stud threads, negative lug clamp face, boot seal lip, or drain-path contamination above the stud, and ties that conclusion to the visual map and measured electrical result. The release boundary is a stud stack with controlled corrosion extent, correct clamp geometry, sealed boots, and no unexplained electrical penalty at the repaired side. |
