Keep the float mechanism and the surrounding sump features visible together so a trapped float can be separated from a wiring or reed-switch fault. |
| T3 | Inspect the float arm for bent travel, sludge pack, tramp-oil drag, tank-wall interference, and contact with the baffle plate through its full upper and lower arc. Compare the arm at low level and at raised level so the boundary lands on float travel, reed-switch response, or tank geometry rather than on the alarm itself. |

If the arm moves freely but continuity does not change, keep the fault on the reed-switch body or connector; if continuity changes correctly by hand but not in the tank, keep the fault on float travel or contamination. |
| T5 | Inspect connector retention, cable-gland sealing, and harness support while the tank is still in the as-found condition. An intermittent terminal or coolant inside the connector can imitate a sticky float, so mechanical and electrical evidence must be read together before the branch is assigned. |
| T6 | Clean or replace only the failed float hardware, restore the baffle clearance and cable support, and confirm that the arm rises and falls without touching the tank wall or trapping under the sludge line. The harness should be held by its clip and gland without pulling the reed-switch body sideways. |
| T7 | Refill to the service level, then rock the machine or circulate coolant through the normal duty cycle while monitoring LS-2 state and tank movement. The repaired float must remain stable through slosh and pump return flow, not only in a motionless sump. |
| T8 | Reinspect the float housing, cable gland, connector, and baffle after the dynamic check because a corrected arm can still stick once the fluid level settles back onto a warped tank wall or a loose cable branch. This second inspection confirms that the restored mechanism survives the real sump environment. |
| T9 | Close the work only after the report names the exact failed boundary, such as sludge-packed float arm, bent arm geometry, reed-switch body, connector retention, or baffle interference, and ties it to the final level-state behavior. Accept the repair when the tank-level signal follows real coolant level repeatably through refill, slosh, and circulation with no false low alarm. |
