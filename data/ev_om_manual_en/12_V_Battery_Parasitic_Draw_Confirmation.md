| T2 | Isolate the 12 V battery branch and expose the positive post clamp, the 250 A mega fuse, the copper feed bar to the front power distribution unit, the clamp bolt, and the protective boot so the full current path from battery post to feed bar can be inspected without changing the original clamp position or the way the boot and cable stack load the terminal. |
| T3 | Inspect the battery post, clamp bore, mega-fuse studs, feed-bar contact lands, and boot interior for heat tint, melted lead, white sulfate, widened clamp gap, or fretting dust that would show the current path has been carrying load across a loose mechanical joint. |
| T4 | Apply a controlled wake load and measure voltage drop from the battery positive post to the feed bar and across the mega-fuse itself, because a battery that tests healthy at rest can still collapse the vehicle if the terminal clamp or fuse interface is generating resistance only under current. |
| T5 | Open the clamp and fuse stack only as far as needed to inspect the hidden contact faces, checking for pitting, oxide film, reduced contact imprint, or cable-crimp heating where the positive cable enters the terminal lug. |
| T6 | Distinguish the failure as loose battery clamp, degraded mega-fuse contact, cable-lug crimp heating, damaged battery post, or no fault in the terminal stack with the voltage drop actually occurring farther downstream at the feed-bar output or front power distribution input, so the boundary stops at one physical interface instead of the whole positive rail. |
| T7 | Finish the record only after the identified boundary is tied to both the visual evidence and the loaded voltage-drop result, and after the same wake or accessory load confirms stable voltage delivery from battery post through mega fuse to feed bar with no repeat reset, so the low-voltage complaint ends on a verified current-path defect instead of a generic weak-battery description. |
