| T1 | Once the Novaris TorqueLine-738 AWD drive unit has been removed from the vehicle, record which interfaces are open to atmosphere, including the two coolant ports, three phase-terminal cavities, resolver socket, breather nipple, axle oil-seal bores, and output-spline ends, before residual fluid is wiped or any temporary cap is installed. |
| T2 | Drain only the trapped coolant and oil that would prevent the plugs from seating, then clean each sealing land, O-ring groove, and machined face with lint-free wipes so no grit is trapped under the orange coolant caps, phase-post insulator covers, or resolver dummy plug. |
| T3 | Fit dedicated protection to each interface by size and function: EPDM caps on the coolant tubes, rigid insulating boots over the U V W phase studs, a keyed blanking plug in the resolver socket, polyethylene sleeves over the output splines, and a vent cap that still allows the breather to equalize without ingesting dust. |
| T4 | Tag every protected port with the matching component location, confirm the caps cannot shake loose when the unit is rotated on the stand, and make sure no tape adhesive touches the machined oil-seal land, connector terminals, or shaft journal that will later become a functional sealing surface. The protection boundary has to stay on the removable caps and sleeves rather than on improvised tape over the actual sealing or contact faces. |
| T5 | Before the drive unit leaves the bay or enters storage, perform a second walk-around with the pallet sheet in hand and verify that each protected interface still matches the list, that no cap has been displaced by handling straps, and that the desiccant bag is secured inside the wrapped assembly rather than left loose. Recheck the lower coolant port and the resolver plug after the unit is tilted on the pallet because those two locations are the most likely to lose protection first during handling. |
| T6 | Release the removed unit only after the paperwork states exactly which port protection was fitted to coolant tubes, phase terminals, resolver socket, breather nipple, oil-seal bores, and output splines, because post-removal contamination control is only traceable when each protected hardware point is named explicitly. The accepted state is a removed drive unit whose open hydraulic, electrical, and rotating interfaces are all protected and still identifiable by location. |
