| T2 | Expose the coolant pump body, its electrical connector, the inlet hose from the reservoir branch, the outlet hose to the manifold, the pump bracket, and the upstream feed path while leaving the hoses in their as-found orientation. |
| T3 | Determine whether the pump issue is electrical or hydraulic by comparing current draw, acoustic response, hose firmness, and return-line movement before any connector or hose is separated, because a silent healthy motor with a blocked feed and a dead motor with an open connector both appear as low flow from live data alone. |
| T4 | Inspect the connector shell, pin drag, rear seal, and the first branch support for moisture or conductor strain, then inspect the inlet hose, clamp line, and high-point bleed path for hose collapse, air ingestion, swollen liner, or restricted reservoir feed. |
| T5 | Command the pump through low and high speed and compare electrical response with actual outlet warm-up and return flow, adding a controlled bleed or hose massage at the high point if trapped air is suspected so the branch behavior is judged under the same condition that caused the original complaint. |
| T6 | Separate the fault only after the evidence points to one boundary such as pump electrical connector, pump motor, inlet restriction, trapped-air feed branch, outlet blockage, or pump-bracket preload that is distorting hose or connector position. |
| T7 | Close the diagnosis with the exact measured condition at pump connector, inlet hose, outlet hose, and return path recorded together, naming one hardware chain as the verified boundary, and if a bleed, hose correction, or connector repair was completed, confirm stable return flow and outlet response through the same commanded speed window before release. |
