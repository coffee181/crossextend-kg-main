| T1 | On Aurex PTC-443 EV, define the ground-path inspection as a follow-up to no heat, heater undervoltage DTC, connector heat witness, or repeated branch-current collapse, and keep any pre-service resistance or voltage-drop reading with the battery state of charge and heater demand condition that produced it. |
| T2 | Make the heater circuit safe and expose the PTC ground stud, copper eyelet, serrated washer stack, body-side land, neighboring HVA280 connector, and the coolant-hose support bracket, preserving washer order and cable exit angle before the first nut is loosened. |
| T3 | Inspect the joint for paint on the seating face, oxide bloom, heat tint at the eyelet barrel, flattened washer teeth, thread pull, or bracket contact that may twist the eyelet out of plane when the heater housing moves under thermal load. |
| T4 | Measure static resistance from heater housing to the body reference point, then repeat the proof as a loaded voltage-drop test during a commanded heat event if the service routine allows it, because the joint can look clean while still failing once heater current rises through the stud and washer stack. |
| T5 | If the reading is unstable or marginal, split the boundary into eyelet face, serrated washer pair, stud shoulder, and body-side land, and inspect each surface for bright-metal coverage, full tooth bite, flat seating, and cable strain so the diagnosis lands on one interface instead of condemning the full branch. |
| T6 | Reassemble only after the contact faces are cleaned, the stud threads are confirmed usable, and the cable lay is restored with proper support, then return the correct washer order and torque and confirm that the eyelet leaves the stud in a neutral line that stays clear of the nearby bracket edge and coolant-hose support. |
| T7 | Release the ground branch only after the repeat loaded proof matches the corrected hardware stack, the voltage drop stays stable during sustained heater demand, and the work order names the accepted or failed boundary at eyelet face, washer stack, stud shoulder, or body-side land. |
