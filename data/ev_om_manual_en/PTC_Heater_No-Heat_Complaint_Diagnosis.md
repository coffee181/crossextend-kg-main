|---|---|
| T1 | On Novaris WarmFlow-634 Plus, record the no-heat complaint together with cabin heat request, PTC enable status, battery state of charge, heater inlet temperature, heater outlet temperature, and any DTC related to heater current, insulation, or coolant circulation before the service bay is opened. |
| T2 | Isolate the high-voltage heater branch, allow the coolant loop to stabilize, and expose the PTC heater housing, orange HV connector, low-voltage control plug, ground stud, inlet hose, outlet hose, bleed point, and the nearby support bracket without disturbing the original hose routing. |
| T3 | Check the thermal side first by confirming reservoir level, electric-pump operation, and hose temperature split at the heater inlet and outlet, because a dead heater and a heater with no coolant exchange can both present as weak cabin heat but belong to different hardware boundaries. |
| T4 | Inspect the HV connector lock, terminal cavity, rear seal, control-plug latch, and heater ground eyelet for water track, heat tint, loose seating, or clamp witness, paying particular attention to the orange connector backshell and the stud washer stack that support the actual current path. |
| T5 | Command the heater on with the approved diagnostic routine and verify that low-voltage enable, HV availability, and heater current rise occur in the expected sequence, then compare the measured current or power value with the reported outlet-temperature response instead of accepting one side of the evidence alone. |
| T6 | If current command is absent, trace the failure through control plug, LIN or PWM enable path, interlock state, and heater feed boundary. If current command is present but no thermal rise follows, separate internal heater-core failure from coolant-side stagnation by watching outlet hose warm-up and branch flow behavior together. |
| T7 | Recheck the branch under a sustained heat request long enough to see whether the complaint is total no-heat, delayed heat, or unstable heat that drops out after the first minute, because several PTC faults appear normal only during the first energization pulse. |
| T8 | Close the diagnosis only after the record names the failure at one hardware boundary such as HV connector seating, control-enable path, ground stud, internal PTC element, trapped-air branch, or no-flow heater outlet circuit, and links that conclusion to both electrical evidence and thermal response, with the final release state recorded as restored current and outlet-hose temperature rise under the same sustained heat request if corrective action was performed. |
