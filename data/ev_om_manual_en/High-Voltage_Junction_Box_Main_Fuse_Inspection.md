| T1 | Capture the trigger for the main-fuse inspection on Trion PowerHub-417 AWD, such as overcurrent history, no-ready event, moisture intrusion, visible discoloration, or a voltage-drop complaint under load, so the fuse check is tied to a known failure context instead of a generic visual review performed with no fault background. Record whether the event followed charging, acceleration, or wash exposure because those conditions point to different fuse and enclosure boundaries. |
| T2 | Switch the vehicle off, isolate the high-voltage system, confirm bus discharge, and open the junction-box cover so the main fuse body, fuse studs, bus bars, terminal nuts, washer stack, insulator plates, and nearby compartment wall are fully exposed for inspection. Keep the full stack visible from stud to bus bar to insulator plate because clamp-load problems are often revealed by the hardware order and seating marks, not by the fuse body alone. |
| T3 | Inspect the fuse body for cracking, swelling, or heat staining, and inspect both fuse studs, bus-bar contact faces, nut-and-washer stack, cover-seal area, and compartment wall for looseness, corrosion, splash residue, or uneven compression witness marks that suggest the real issue is in the hardware stack rather than in the fuse element alone. Compare the inlet and outlet sides of the fuse so one-sided heat tint or moisture residue is tied to one stud interface instead of to the component generally. |
| T4 | Remove only the hardware needed to inspect the fuse contact surfaces directly, keeping the washer order indexed by position so the original stack can be compared with the intended build sequence during reassembly or escalation to replacement. Inspect the underside of the bus-bar lugs and the first exposed stud threads for pitting, arc shadow, or flattened plating that would explain abnormal drop even if continuity remains present. |
| T5 | Measure continuity or resistance drop across the main fuse and compare it with the expected low-loss value, making sure the probe points sit on clean metal at the fuse studs and not on contaminated thread, coated washer faces, or heat-discolored nut surfaces. If a load-capable check is available, compare the drop against the opposite healthy high-current joint so the measured loss can be assigned to the fuse element or to the contact stack. |
| T6 | Inspect the nearby cover seal, drain path, and compartment floor for moisture residue or splash marks that could explain corrosion, tracking, or progressive clamp-loss at the fuse hardware even if the fuse element itself still measures electrically intact. The moisture assessment has to show whether contamination entered from above the cover seal, from the side wall, or from trapped fluid at the compartment floor below the fuse. |
| T7 | Reassemble the fuse hardware stack in order or tag the fuse for replacement if the body, studs, or contact faces exceed reuse limits, and name the actual issue specifically as main fuse open, fuse-stud corrosion, bus-bar hardware heat damage, or moisture-driven contact degradation. The fault boundary should stop at one element or one stud stack, not remain at generic junction-box concern. |
| T8 | Close the box and complete the inspection only after the record includes fuse-body condition, stud condition, resistance result, hardware order, moisture-path observations, and the final disposition of the main fuse hardware at component level. The release condition is a junction-box record that identifies exactly which fuse interface was inspected, what was measured there, and whether the fuse stack was accepted or rejected. |
