| T1 | On Novaris WarmFlow-672 AWD, log the customer complaint as weak cabin heat after remote preconditioning, delayed windshield defog, or heater shutdown under load, and capture the active DTC set, battery state of charge, ambient temperature, coolant reservoir level, and whether the Bosch EWP48 coolant pump was already running when the vehicle entered the bay. |
| T2 | Isolate the high-voltage heater branch, wait for the orange cable and coolant loop to reach a serviceable state, and expose the BorgWarner PTC module PTM-7.2, the HVA280-2P high-voltage connector, the Aptiv MX150-6P low-voltage control plug, the M8 heater ground stud, the bleed screw, and both 19 mm EPDM coolant hoses without changing the original routing. |
| T3 | Establish whether the complaint belongs to the coolant side or the electrical side by checking reservoir level, pump command, inlet-hose temperature, outlet-hose temperature, and the presence of flow noise at the heater body before any connector is unplugged, because a cold outlet with valid current request is a different boundary from a heater that never receives current. |
| T4 | Inspect the HVA280 connector body, CPA lock, rear seal, pin cavities, and backshell for soot, heat tint, melted keyways, or witness marks from partial seating, then compare that evidence with the washer stack and torque stripe at the M8 ground stud because elevated contact resistance often leaves traces at both ends of the current path. |
| T5 | Verify the control side separately by checking ignition wake, heater enable command, LIN communication on the MX150-6P plug, and the low-voltage ground reference, so the diagnosis does not confuse a missing command from the thermal controller with a failed PTC element or damaged high-voltage feed. |
| T6 | Re-enable the branch under the approved service routine and monitor heater current, voltage availability, outlet-hose warm-up rate, and the time delay between command and thermal response, recording whether the branch shows no current, current with no temperature rise, or current that rises briefly and collapses after the first energization pulse. |
| T7 | If the heater current is unstable, trace the fault upstream through the HVA280 terminal grip, the BDU heater fuse path, and the ground stud clamping surfaces. If current stays normal but outlet temperature remains flat, separate trapped air, blocked coolant exchange, and internal heater-core failure by watching pump behavior, bleed response, and inlet-to-outlet delta together. |
| T8 | Repeat the test after any connector reseating or coolant bleed action, holding the heat request long enough to cover steady-state operation rather than the first thirty seconds, because several heater branches appear normal during initial warm-up and fail only when connector temperature or coolant demand rises. |
| T9 | Close the record only after the fault is named at one hardware boundary such as HVA280 partial seating, MX150 control loss, M8 ground stud resistance, internal PTM-7.2 heater failure, trapped-air outlet branch, or no-flow coolant circuit, and link that conclusion to both electrical evidence and measured thermal behavior, with the final release condition recorded as restored heater current and stable outlet-hose temperature rise under the same heat request if corrective action was performed. |
