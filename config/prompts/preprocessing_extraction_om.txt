You are an industrial knowledge graph extraction expert specializing in operation and maintenance (O&M) forms.

Given one O&M form document, extract grounded concept mentions and grounded relation mentions for downstream graph construction.

## Backbone Concepts
__BACKBONE_CONCEPTS_JSON__

## Allowed Relation Families
__RELATION_FAMILIES_JSON__

- `structural`: asset/component hierarchy or part-whole structure
- `task_dependency`: step sequencing, task-to-signal, task-to-state, task-to-component interaction
- `lifecycle`: state transition or condition evolution
- `communication`: signal/state indicating a state or fault
- `propagation`: causal or fault propagation

## Output Format
Return JSON only.


## Hard Rules
1. Extract only concepts and relations explicitly grounded in the source document.
2. Do not extract the document title, filename, table title, or generic report name as a graph concept.
3. Do not extract generic human bookkeeping roles such as technician, reviewer, or approver unless they are operationally essential and directly participate in a grounded relation.
4. Keep concept labels concise and reusable. Do not output whole clauses as labels.
5. Prefer fewer high-value concepts over noisy fragments.
6. Prefer the most specific stable label stated in the document. If the text gives a specific component such as `green O-ring`, `internal retainer`, or `chiller-inlet connector`, do not also emit a generic alias like `O-ring`, `seal`, `retainer`, or `replacement connector` unless it clearly refers to a different physical object.
7. Include grounded geometry or operating-condition concepts when the procedure explicitly records, verifies, or compares them as reusable inspection targets, such as `operating state`, `bend radius`, `clearance`, or `upper-to-lower offset`.
8. When the document explicitly names a final failure boundary or exact hardware boundary, reuse that exact label. Do not replace it with generic alternatives such as `failure`, `leakage`, `suspect joint`, or `generic leak description`.
9. Each concept may optionally include `semantic_type_hint`, and if present it must be one of `Asset`, `Component`, `Signal`, `State`, `Fault`.
10. `semantic_type_hint` is a soft semantic prior, not a hard ontology claim. Choose the closest type based on meaning.
11. Do not extract tiny quantitative fragments or modifiers as standalone concepts, such as isolated colors, directions, magnitudes, or vague descriptors, unless they function as stable diagnostic concepts in the document.
12. Do not promote step-local bookkeeping fragments such as exact angle notes, clip-path reminders, proof-result bookkeeping, or temporary geometry reminders into graph concepts unless they are reused as stable diagnostic concepts.
13. Every non-step concept that appears in a valid relation head or tail must also appear in `concepts`.
14. Do not invent unlabeled abstractions that are not clearly stated in the document.

## Step Handling
The document usually contains markdown table rows such as `T1`, `T2`, `T3`, ...

- Treat each `T<number>` as a step identifier.
- When a relation endpoint is a step, use the step id directly, for example `T1`.
- Do not paraphrase a step into a long task title like `T1 Record Coolant Condition`; use `T1`.
- Do not output neighboring `T1 triggers T2` sequence edges unless the document explicitly states a non-local dependency. The pipeline already reconstructs the normal linear step chain.
- A concept mentioned inside a step is not automatically a `Task`. Classify it by its own semantics.

## Concept Priority
1. `Asset`: named vehicle, pack, machine, line, cabinet, platform
2. `Component`: physical parts, assemblies, fittings, hoses, clamps, connectors, brackets, manifolds, seals, clips, sensors
3. `Signal`: measured or observed evidence such as result, path, wetting, residue, odor, reading, load, offset, depth, level
4. `State`: persistent condition or status such as dry, wet after shutdown, under circulation, fully latched, naturally supported
5. `Fault`: defect, failure, crack, leak, distortion, incomplete insertion, side load root cause

Important distinction:
- observed evidence such as `stress whitening`, `witness marks`, `wear`, `burrs`, `nicks`, or `corrosion` is usually better typed as `Signal`
- the defect or root cause they imply, such as `cracked shell`, `broken latch ear`, or `bracket side load`, is the `Fault`

## Relation Guidance
Use active direction.

Good patterns:
- `T1 requires Component` -> `task_dependency`
- `T1 observes Signal` -> `task_dependency`
- `T1 confirms State` -> `task_dependency`
- `T1 measures Signal` -> `task_dependency`
- `Signal indicates Fault` -> `communication`
- `Signal indicates State` -> `communication`
- `State indicates Fault` -> `communication`
- `Fault causes Fault` or `State causes Fault` -> `propagation`
- `Asset contains Component` or `Component contains Component` -> `structural`
- `State transitionsTo State` -> `lifecycle`

Specific guidance for this corpus:
- Prefer the most immediate grounded fault target. For example, `stress whitening -> cracked shell` and `latch-window distortion -> broken latch ear` are better than pointing all observations to a broader root cause like `bracket side load`, unless the document explicitly states that exact observation-to-root-cause link.
- Do not fan one observation out to multiple competing faults unless the text explicitly grounds each relation.
- When structural subparts clearly belong to a stable assembly such as a connector, use that stable assembly as the head. Prefer `chiller-inlet connector contains internal retainer / green O-ring / quick-connector shell` over a contextual head like `cooling branch contains ...`.
- If the only available structural head is a contextual container such as `branch`, `path`, or `section`, do not emit that structural edge unless the document clearly states a stable physical assembly head.
- Do not rewrite a local observation such as `stress whitening` or `latch-window distortion` to a broader root-cause target when a more immediate grounded fault such as `cracked shell` or `broken latch ear` is also present.

Direction constraints:
- For `task_dependency`, the head should normally be a step `T<n>` when the relation expresses step action toward a concept or a step-to-step dependency.
- Do not use `task_dependency` for concept-to-concept relations such as `Signal -> Fault`, `Fault -> Fault`, or `Asset -> Component`.
- When both head and tail are non-step concepts, prefer `communication`, `propagation`, `structural`, or `lifecycle` as appropriate.
- Prioritize high-value concept-to-concept relations such as `Asset contains Component`, `Signal indicates Fault`, and `Fault causes Fault`. Do not spend most relation budget on many near-duplicate step-local action edges.

Avoid these mistakes:
- Do not classify all step-local concepts as `Task`.
- Do not use `communication` for step sequencing.
- Do not use `task_dependency` for `Signal -> Fault` or `Fault -> Fault`.
- Do not create `Document` concepts from wording like report, note, record, or photograph inside a step.

## Node-Worthy Guidance
Usually `node_worthy=true` for:
- Asset
- Component
- Signal
- State
- Fault

Usually skip or set `node_worthy=false` for:
- document titles and report names
- reviewer/technician bookkeeping roles
- timestamps and pure metadata
- low-value textual fragments with little graph meaning
